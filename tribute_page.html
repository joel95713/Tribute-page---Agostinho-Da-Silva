Agostinho da Silva was a philosopher, poet, essayist, professor, philologist, pedagogue, and translator. His thought combined elements of pantheism, millenarianism, and an ethic of renunciation, affirming freedom as the most important quality of human beings. He is considered one of the most important Portuguese intellectuals of the 20th century. Among his extensive bibliography, his book “Sete Cartas a um Jovem Filósofo” (“Seven Letters to a Young Philosopher”), published in 1945, stands out. He also collaborated with several periodicals, including the magazines “57” and "Princípio".

Here’s a timeline of Agostinho da Silva’s life:

    • 1906 - February 13: Born in the Bonfim district, Porto, Portugal.
    • 1920s: Begins studies at the Faculty of Letters, University of Coimbra, showing interest in Philosophy and Humanities.
    • 1929: Completes studies in Classical Philology and starts teaching at secondary schools in Portugal.
    • 1930: Receives a scholarship to study at the University of Paris, where he's influenced by philosophers like Henri Bergson.
    • 1933: Returns to Portugal and starts teaching Philosophy at the University of Lisbon.
    • 1940: Due to his critical opinions about the Estado Novo regime, he's arrested by the political police and later goes into exile in Brazil.
    • 1945 - 1960s: In Brazil, works as a professor at the Federal University of Minas Gerais and actively contributes to the intellectual development of various thinkers.
    • 1950s: Publishes numerous essays and books, including "Um Oráculo para Aquiles" ("An Oracle for Achilles"), exploring philosophical and spiritual themes.
    • 1969: Returns to Portugal for the first time after his exile, and his philosophical ideals continue to influence young and open minds.
    • 1974: With the Carnation Revolution, Agostinho da Silva definitively returns to Portugal.
    • 1994 - April 3: Passes away in Lisbon, leaving behind a legacy of profoundly humanistic and spiritual thought.

-- Agostinho da Silva
